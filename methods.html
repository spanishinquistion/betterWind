Predicted Wind Speed Average at Hub Height

Our preliminary analysis provides wind speed values projected to three standard turbine hub heights. Wind speed is predictably generally faster higher up than near the ground. The average wind speed is scaled up from the measurement height to the hub height using an industry-standard logarithmic wind profile.

This profile works an assumed roughness length of z0 = 0.05, which assumes the site is flat with minimal blockage, such as rural croplands. Obstacles at a site increase the surface roughness and, in turn, lower the speed of the wind as height increases.

Logarithmic profile used for the analysis

$$v_2 = v_1 { ln ( h_2 / z_0 ) \over ln ( h_1 / z_0)}$$

Term Definition
v2 wind speed projected to turbine hub height
v1 wind speed at measurement height
h2 turbine hub height
h1 measurement height
z0 surface roughness

Wind Speed Probability Distribution

Wind speed varies at a given site; the industry standard that best models wind speeds is the Weibull distribution. This model allows for a complete estimation of the wind speed probability distribution.

Since the power produced by a wind turbine varies with the wind speed, it is necessary to calculate the wind speed distribution for all wind speeds where a turbine produces power, thus creating a power curve. This data allows us to estimate the energy production and capacity factor.

Capacity Factor Cp %

The capacity factor is the percentage estimate of the potential energy a wind turbine produces at a given site for a typical year. If a turbine were to produce at its rated or maximum power over an entire year, then its capacity factor would be 100%. Most onshore sites in Germany expect values in the low teens.

Capacity Factor is a valuable data point for estimating the production and economic viability of a turbine at a site.

The Power Curve P

The power curve is the amount of power a wind turbine produces at specific wind speeds. The maximum is the rated power of the turbine, but wind turbine power production is dependent upon the wind speed. Our formula, shown below, calculates power production factoring in standard turbine specifications and wind speed probability distribution.

Profile used for power curve

$$P = 1/2 \cdot C_p \cdot \rho \cdot \pi \cdot R^2 \cdot v^3$$

Estimated Power Production

With a site wind speed distribution vector and a power curve vector from a turbine, you can estimate the power production.

The turbines used in the power analysis represent three turbine size categories. The smallest turbine [S] is applicable for a home or farm. The medium turbine [M] is based on a commercial pitch-controlled turbine. The large turbine [L] is also based on a pitch-controlled model and is near the maximum size for onshore installations. The data for the turbine models are in the table below.
